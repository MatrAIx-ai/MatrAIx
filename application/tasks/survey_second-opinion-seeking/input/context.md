# Seeking a Second Medical Opinion

Consumer attitudes, experiences, and decision factors around seeking second medical opinions.

## About this survey

This survey measures whether respondents have ever sought a second opinion, how many times, and their general openness to doing so, then explores the situations that would prompt one and how they would find another provider. It captures the outcome of past second opinions and what people did next, along with the barriers of cost, time, distance, and not wanting to offend a doctor. Attitudinal Likert batteries cover normalization, offense worry, added confidence, worth of time and cost, and trust in remote opinions, and a rated importance matrix scores factors in choosing a second-opinion provider such as credentials, coverage, speed, top-center affiliation, and remote availability. Later sections examine treatment delay, interest in virtual second-opinion services, insurance influence, willingness to pay, a top-3 ranking of encouragers, perceived value, future likelihood, and an optional open comment. It uses single-choice, multi-select, Likert and importance scales, a matrix battery, and numeric free-text, and closes with demographics covering age, gender, race and ethnicity, education, insurance status, self-rated health, employment, household size, region, urbanicity, and income.
